Texas Property Tax Reassessments Are Coming in August — Should You Buy Before Then?

Credit & FinanceBy Bond Peter NjokuJuly 13, 20269 min read

I'm Bond Peter Njoku, and this is a question I'm hearing more this month: should I try to close on my house before the next round of county property tax reassessments hits in August? It's a smart question to ask, because in Texas, property taxes are one of the biggest ongoing costs of owning a home — often bigger than most buyers expect when they're only focused on the purchase price and interest rate. Here's what the reassessment timeline actually means for you, and what genuinely matters versus what doesn't.

How Texas Property Tax Reassessments Actually Work

Texas county appraisal districts reassess property values annually, with certified values and local taxing entity rate discussions typically wrapping up in the late summer — often around August — ahead of tax bills going out in the fall. Because Texas has no state income tax, property taxes fund a larger share of local services here than in most states, which is part of why rates commonly run in the 1.8% to 2.5%+ range depending on your county, school district, and any MUD or PID assessments layered on top.

Does Buying Before August Actually Save You Money?

Here's the honest answer: closing before or after a reassessment doesn't change how your specific property gets valued going forward — the appraisal district assesses based on market value regardless of who owns it or when they bought it. What timing does affect is which tax year's bill you and the seller are prorating at closing, and how your first year of escrow gets estimated. If a home hasn't been reassessed in a while and comparable sales have pushed values up, buying now versus after the new certified values post could change what your lender initially escrows for taxes — which affects your monthly payment estimate, even if it doesn't change your long-term tax bill.

What You Should Actually Do Instead of Racing a Deadline

  • Get your full monthly payment estimate up front. I include property taxes, not just principal and interest, in every pre-approval so there are no surprises.
  • File your homestead exemption immediately after closing. This is one of the biggest tax reductions available to Texas homeowners and it's easy to forget.
  • Ask about the specific school district and MUD rates for any home you're considering. These vary significantly even between neighboring subdivisions.
  • Don't rush a purchase just to beat a reassessment date. The right home and the right financing matter more than a few weeks of timing.
