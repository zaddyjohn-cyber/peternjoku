What Debt-to-Income Ratio Do You Need for a Mortgage in Texas?

Mortgage Tips By Peter June 28, 2026 5 min read

Your credit score gets a lot of attention when people talk about mortgage qualifying — but your debt-to-income ratio (DTI) is equally important, and often less understood. A buyer with a 750 credit score and a 55% DTI may not qualify for the home they want. A buyer with a 640 score and a 38% DTI often has no problem. Here's what DTI actually means, how it's calculated, what lenders want to see in Texas, and how to improve yours.

What Is Debt-to-Income Ratio?

DTI is a simple formula: your total monthly debt obligations divided by your gross monthly income (before taxes), expressed as a percentage.

DTI = Total Monthly Debts ÷ Gross Monthly Income × 100

Lenders use it to gauge how much of your income is already committed to debt payments — and whether adding a mortgage payment leaves you with enough financial breathing room to be a reliable borrower.

Front-End vs. Back-End DTI

There are actually two DTI numbers lenders look at:

  • Front-end DTI (also called the housing ratio): housing costs only — your proposed mortgage payment including principal, interest, property taxes, homeowner's insurance, and HOA dues if applicable. This is sometimes referred to as PITI. Most loan programs want this below 28–31%.
  • Back-end DTI (the number everyone means when they say "DTI"): all monthly debt obligations — housing payment plus car loans, student loans, minimum credit card payments, personal loans, child support, alimony. This is the number that has program caps.

When a lender says "your DTI is too high," they almost always mean the back-end DTI. That's the one to focus on.

DTI Limits by Loan Type in Texas

Different programs have different thresholds:

  • FHA Loans: FHA's official guideline targets a back-end DTI of 43%, but with strong compensating factors (higher credit score, larger down payment, significant reserves) lenders can approve DTI up to 50% or occasionally higher through automated underwriting. FHA is generally the most flexible on DTI.
  • Conventional Loans: Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac allow back-end DTI up to 45% through their automated systems; some files with very strong profiles may go to 50%. In practice, lenders get more cautious above 43–45%.
  • VA Loans: The VA doesn't impose a hard DTI cap, but 41% is the preferred threshold. Files above 41% require additional documentation that the borrower has sufficient residual income (money left over after all obligations) to cover daily expenses.
  • USDA Loans: USDA targets a back-end DTI of 41%, though it can go to 44–45% with compensating factors through the automated underwriting system.

A Real DTI Calculation Example

Let's walk through an example with a Texas buyer earning $5,000/month gross income:

  • Car loan payment: $400/month
  • Student loan payment: $100/month
  • Proposed mortgage payment (PITI): $1,400/month

Total monthly debts: $400 + $100 + $1,400 = $1,900

Back-end DTI: $1,900 ÷ $5,000 = 38%

A 38% DTI is well within guidelines for FHA, conventional, VA, and USDA loans. Now change the scenario slightly — add a $300/month personal loan payment. Total debts become $2,200 and DTI becomes 44%. That still qualifies for FHA and conventional, but the file gets more scrutiny. Add another $200/month credit card minimum and DTI hits 48% — now we're at the edge of FHA flexibility and beyond most conventional guidelines without strong compensating factors.

What Counts as "Debt" for DTI Purposes?

Lenders count recurring monthly obligations that appear on your credit report or can be documented:

  • Car loans and leases
  • Student loans (even if deferred — lenders often use a percentage of the balance)
  • Minimum credit card payments
  • Personal loans
  • Child support and alimony (court-ordered)
  • Other installment debts

What lenders do NOT count toward DTI:

  • Utilities (electricity, gas, water)
  • Cell phone bills
  • Health insurance premiums (unless deducted from pay)
  • Subscriptions and memberships
  • Grocery or food expenses

This is why paying off a car loan before applying can make a meaningful difference — it eliminates a real DTI line item — while canceling your streaming services has no effect on what the lender sees.

Why Texas Property Taxes Matter for Your DTI

Texas has no state income tax, which is a financial advantage in many ways. But the tradeoff is higher property tax rates — typically ranging from 1.8% to 2.5% of assessed value annually depending on the county and city. For a $350,000 home, that's $525–$729 per month in property taxes alone, rolled into your PITI payment.

Because property taxes are included in the housing payment used to calculate your front-end and back-end DTI, Texas buyers effectively carry a higher housing payment — and therefore a higher DTI — than buyers of comparable homes in lower-tax states. This means a Texas buyer may need a slightly higher income to qualify for the same loan amount as a buyer in a state with lower property taxes. How to Lower Your DTI Before Applying

There are two levers: reduce debt or increase income. Here are the most effective specific actions:

  • Pay off small balances entirely. Eliminating a car payment or a credit card completely removes that monthly obligation from the DTI calculation. Reducing a balance without eliminating it has no DTI impact.
  • Avoid new debt. Taking out a new car loan or credit card right before applying adds monthly obligations and can tank your qualifying amount. Hold off on any new credit until after closing.
  • Document all income sources. A part-time job, freelance income, rental income, or overtime can increase your denominator. Lenders typically require a 2-year history for self-employment income; W-2 overtime can be counted if it's documented as consistent.
  • Add a co-borrower. If a spouse or co-buyer has income, combining it raises the denominator. Be aware that both credit profiles will be reviewed.
  • Target the right loan program. Working with a broker means we can match you to the program whose DTI guidelines best fit your specific file — rather than being limited to one bank's overlays.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a good debt-to-income ratio for a mortgage in Texas?

A DTI below 36% is considered strong by most lenders. However, you can still qualify for many loan programs with a higher DTI — FHA typically allows up to 43–50% and sometimes higher with compensating factors, conventional up to 45–50% with strong credit, and VA has no hard cap though 41% is the preferred threshold. The lower your DTI, the more purchasing power you have and the better your rate options.

What counts toward my DTI for a mortgage?

Lenders count your recurring monthly debt obligations: car loans, student loans, minimum credit card payments, personal loans, child support or alimony, and the proposed new mortgage payment (principal, interest, taxes, insurance, and HOA if applicable). They do NOT count utilities, cell phone bills, health insurance premiums, or subscriptions.

Why do Texas property taxes affect DTI?

Texas has relatively high property tax rates — typically 1.8% to 2.5% of assessed value annually. Those taxes are included in your monthly PITI payment. Because property taxes are higher in Texas, the monthly housing payment used to calculate DTI is also higher for the same home price, meaning your effective DTI may be higher than buyers in lower-tax states at the same income and loan amount.

How can I lower my DTI before applying for a mortgage in Texas?

The most effective strategies are: pay off the smallest debt balances first to eliminate those monthly obligations entirely, avoid taking on any new debt, and if possible document additional income sources such as a part-time job, freelance work, or rental income. You can also apply with a co-borrower whose income helps the ratio. Talking to a mortgage professional before applying lets you target the specific debts that will move the needle most.
