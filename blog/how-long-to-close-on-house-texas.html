How Long Does It Take to Close on a House in Texas?

Home Buying By Peter June 28, 2026 6 min read

One of the first questions buyers ask after getting a contract accepted is: how soon can we close? The answer depends on your loan type, how clean your file is, and how quickly everyone in the transaction moves. Here's an honest, stage-by-stage breakdown of the Texas closing timeline — and exactly what you can do to speed it up.

The Texas Closing Timeline at a Glance

For most buyers in the DFW area, here's what to expect:

  • Conventional loans: 30–45 days typical; as fast as 21 days with a strong pre-approved file
  • FHA loans: 45–60 days due to stricter appraisal requirements and additional inspections
  • VA loans: 45–60 days; VA appraisals (called tidewater appraisals) can add time
  • USDA loans: 45–60 days, sometimes longer due to USDA's own guarantee processing step

These are from offer acceptance to closing day. If you're not yet pre-approved, add another 1–3 business days at the front end.

Stage-by-Stage Breakdown of the Texas Closing Process

Pre-Approval (1–3 business days): Before you make an offer, get fully pre-approved — not just pre-qualified. This means your income, assets, and credit have all been verified. A fully pre-approved buyer moves through underwriting significantly faster because much of the work is already done.

Offer Accepted — Option Period (days 1–10): Texas real estate contracts typically include an option period of 5 to 10 days. During this window, you pay a small option fee for the unrestricted right to back out of the deal for any reason. This is when you order and complete your home inspection and negotiate any repairs with the seller. Your lender simultaneously orders the appraisal during this window — don't wait.

Home Inspection (days 3–10): A licensed inspector evaluates the property's condition. This usually takes 2–3 hours and the report comes back within 24 hours. If repairs are needed, negotiating them can take a few additional days. Major issues may require re-inspection.

Appraisal (days 7–21): The lender orders an independent appraisal to confirm the home's market value supports the loan amount. In active markets like DFW, appraisers are often busy and the turnaround can be 10–14 days. If the appraisal comes in low, there's an additional negotiation step with the seller or the buyer must cover the gap.

Underwriting (days 7–21 after receiving the full file): The underwriter reviews your entire loan package — income documentation, credit, appraisal, title, insurance, and property details. They may issue a list of conditions that must be satisfied before final approval. How quickly you respond to those conditions directly controls how fast this stage ends.

Clear to Close (24–72 hours before closing): Once the underwriter is satisfied with all conditions, they issue a Clear to Close (CTC). The title company prepares your closing disclosure, which you must receive at least 3 business days before signing. This is a federal requirement and cannot be rushed.

Closing Day: You'll sign a stack of documents at the title company, pay your closing costs, and receive the keys. In Texas, closings are handled by title companies — not attorneys — which makes them generally efficient. The process usually takes 1–2 hours.

What Causes Closing Delays in Texas?

Most delays are predictable and preventable. The most common culprits:

  • Appraisal issues. A low appraisal requires renegotiation or an appraisal appeal, which can add days or weeks. In fast-moving markets like Frisco, McKinney, and Garland, this happens more often than buyers expect.
  • Title problems. Outstanding liens, boundary disputes, or errors in the public record must be cleared before a lender will fund. Some title issues take days; others can take weeks.
  • Missing documents from the buyer. If the underwriter requests a bank statement or letter of explanation and the buyer takes five days to respond, that's five days added to the timeline.
  • Major financial changes during the process. Buying a car, changing jobs, or opening a new credit card after your offer is accepted can trigger a full re-underwrite.
  • Lender backlogs. During peak purchase seasons (spring and early summer), high-volume lenders can back up significantly. Mortgage brokers with access to multiple wholesale lenders can often route your file to a lender with faster turn times.

How to Speed Up Your Texas Closing

Here's what actually works:

  • Get fully pre-approved before you start making offers — not just pre-qualified
  • Respond to every document request within 24 hours. Faster response = faster closing
  • Order the appraisal immediately after the option period begins
  • Don't make any new financial moves after the contract is signed
  • Choose a title company that's efficient and familiar with DFW transactions
  • Work with a mortgage broker who actively manages your file and chases the lender on your behalf

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the average time to close on a house in Texas?

The average closing timeline in Texas is 30 to 45 days from the accepted offer date for most conventional loans. FHA, VA, and USDA loans typically run 45 to 60 days due to additional appraisal and inspection requirements. Buyers with a clean file who get pre-approved before shopping can sometimes close in as few as 21 days on a conventional loan.

What is the option period in a Texas real estate contract?

The option period is a Texas-specific contract feature that gives the buyer an unrestricted right to terminate the contract for any reason — typically lasting 5 to 10 days. The buyer pays a small option fee (usually $100–$500) for this right. During the option period, inspections are conducted and the buyer can negotiate repairs or back out without losing their earnest money deposit.

What causes a mortgage closing to be delayed in Texas?

The most common causes of closing delays are a low or disputed property appraisal, title issues that need to be resolved (such as liens or ownership discrepancies), slow response from the buyer on document requests, major financial changes by the buyer during the process (like taking on new debt), and lender backlogs during busy purchase seasons. Getting fully pre-approved before making an offer is the single best way to reduce delay risk.
