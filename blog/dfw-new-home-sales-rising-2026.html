DFW New Home Sales Are Rising — What Falling Builder Prices Mean for Your Mortgage

Market UpdateBy Bond Peter NjokuJuly 14, 20267 min read

I'm Bond Peter Njoku, and there's an interesting shift happening in the DFW new construction market right now: sales volume is climbing while builder prices are actually falling. New home closings across the Metroplex picked up meaningfully this spring compared to the month before, even as the average price per new home dropped by roughly $10,000 compared to a year earlier. That combination — more sales, lower prices — doesn't happen often, and it's worth understanding if you're weighing new construction against a resale home.

Why Builders Are Cutting Prices While Sales Rise

Builders in DFW have been sitting on completed and near-complete inventory for a while now, and with rates holding in the mid-6% range, they've leaned hard into price reductions and incentives — rate buydowns, closing cost credits, design upgrades thrown in at no charge — rather than waiting for buyers to accept sticker price. It's working. Lower effective prices are pulling hesitant buyers off the sidelines, which is why sales volume is rising even as headline prices fall.

What This Means If You're Comparing New vs. Existing

The price gap between new construction and resale homes has narrowed in a lot of DFW submarkets. A few things to weigh:

  • Builder incentives often beat resale negotiating room. A builder-funded 2-1 rate buydown can lower your effective payment more than a comparable price cut on a resale home.
  • New homes come with lower near-term maintenance costs. No immediate roof, HVAC, or appliance replacements to budget for.
  • Resale homes are often in more established neighborhoods with mature landscaping and shorter commutes to city centers, which matters if location outweighs "new."
  • You are never required to use the builder's in-house lender — shop your loan even if you love the builder incentive package.

Financing a New Construction Purchase

Most DFW new builds finance with a standard FHA, VA, or conventional loan once the home is complete or near completion — the same programs you'd use for a resale home. If you're building custom on your own lot, that's a different animal requiring a construction-to-permanent loan, and I'd want to talk through that separately since the underwriting is more involved. For most production-builder purchases in places like Forney, McKinney, or Frisco, financing works the same as any other purchase.
